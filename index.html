Core Surge Model
Type: Empirical probability model · hour × day-of-week × month lookup, blended with school calendar and ILI modifier
Outcome: P(≥threshold arrivals in a single hour) — threshold adjustable 6–15/hr
AUC: 0.838 (default t=10) · N=146,276 visits · 3-year training set
Peak hours: 17:00–20:00 · Peak days: Mon–Wed · Peak months: Jan–Mar, Sep–Oct
School effect: School days shift surge window to 17:00–21:00. School breaks reduce daily volume ~5% but increase off-peak hours. Post-break return adds ×1.15–1.25 modifier.
Surge window reduction: Hours 15–21 receive empirical decay multipliers when no surge has been confirmed — P(surge at H | no surge since 15:00) drops monotonically each passing hour.
Surge Persistence · Volume-Stratified Survival
Activation: When surge is confirmed (YES button), forecast bars switch from the calendar model to empirical survival probabilities for h+1 through h+6.
Volume stratification: Survival tables are stratified by arrival volume at the confirmed surge hour relative to the active threshold (t). Staff enter the current hour's arrival count using the "Arrivals This Hour" stepper in Current Status.
LOWt to t+1/hrJust-threshold surge — shortest persistence (e.g. 30% h+1 at t=10, 18:00 start) MODERATEt+2 to t+4/hrModerate surge — intermediate persistence (62% h+1) HIGHt+5+/hrStrong surge — highest persistence (69% h+1)
Statistical basis: ANOVA F=4.25, p=0.0007 across volume bands at onset. Point-biserial r=0.12 (p<0.0001) for vol@onset vs h+1 survival. Analysis of 4,241 surge events (t=10), 2023–2025. Intra-episode ICC=-0.41, confirming independence assumption is conservative (effective n=5,709 > nominal).
Backtime stepper: Adjust surge start hour back up to 6 hours if surge began earlier than the current hour. Shifting the start hour changes the survival lookup key — earlier starts use their own empirical persistence curves.
Horizon: 6 hours (h+1 through h+6). Beyond h+6, persistence is near zero for all peak-hour starts — bars revert to the calendar model.
Zero-event hours (e.g. 02:00–07:00 at t≥10) fall back to overall survival. Confirming a surge at these hours triggers a data-quality warning. Minimum n=5 events per cell required; cells below threshold default to 0.

Mental Health Model
Method: Empirical (hour × DOW × month) expected visit rate with Poisson P(≥1) for bar display
Peak hours: 10:00–22:00 · Peak days: Thu/Sat · Peak months: Mar–May, Sep–Oct
LOS reference: Discharge median 5.0h · Admit median 5.8h · Transfer median 17.7h · 90th pct 24.9h
LOS pattern: Morning arrivals (08:00) avg 8.9h vs late evening (21:00) avg 20.8h
MH autocorrelation: RR ×1.30 lag+1, ×1.25 lag+7 (weekly rhythm) at ≥10/day threshold. Entered via "Yesterday's MH" slider on the MH tab. At ≥8/day yesterday: +25% today and +32% same day next week. At ≥10/day: +30% today, +25% same day next week.
Above Threshold stat: Shows today's estimated MH visits as a percentage of the user-set surge threshold (dailyEst ÷ threshold × 100%). A value of 100% means the estimate equals the threshold; >100% means it exceeds it. The threshold is adjustable via the "MH Surge ≥" slider (default 8/day). Historical base rate at each threshold is shown below the slider for context.
Weather: Null for MH after month+DOW adjustment — all variables tested (sunshine, temperature, precipitation, wind, dark streaks) returned r<0.07, all p>0.02. Same-day and lags 1–14 days. MH visits are driven by social rhythms and accumulated distress, not same-day meteorology. No weather modifier applied.

Weather Outlook · Injury & CC Impact Model
Purpose: Provides a 7-day weather forecast with empirically derived estimates of injury visit volume impact, displayed as a staffing advisory on the main dashboard. Fetches live from Open-Meteo (SEA-TAC coordinates: 47.4444, −122.3138) on each page load.
Key Finding
Anomalously sunny days — defined as above the within-month p75 sunshine threshold — are associated with elevated injury visit counts in spring and fall only. Summer is null (virtually no non-sunny comparison days). Winter is null (cold suppresses outdoor activity despite sunshine). Temperature alone has no independent injury signal after season is controlled.
Empirical Signals (N=146,164 visits · 2023–2025)

Classification Logic
Each forecast day is classified using within-month p75 thresholds for both sunshine hours and max temperature, derived from SEA-TAC 2023–2025 historical data. A day is "anomalously sunny" only if it exceeds the p75 for that specific month — a sunny February (threshold: 8.6h) triggers the signal where a sunny July (threshold: 15.0h) does not. This prevents false positives from summer baseline sunshine being flagged as anomalous.
What the Signal Is — and Isn't
The effect is on absolute injury count, not injury rate. On anomalously sunny spring days, overall ED volume also rises slightly — injury share as a percentage of total visits shifts by ~2–3 percentage points. The clinical observation (more injuries on nice days) is correct; the signal was previously masked by over-adjustment and using rate rather than count as the outcome. The effect is not large enough to shift hourly surge probability — it diffuses across 15+ hours — but it does affect evening case mix (16:00–22:00 window) and is operationally relevant for ortho, suture, and imaging capacity planning.
Analysis basis: partial Spearman correlation on within-month sunshine anomaly vs daily injury count, N=1,091 days. Season-stratified MWU tests. Investigation prompted by clinical observation that sunny days appear to bring more injuries — confirmed using anomaly approach after initial month-adjusted analysis incorrectly found null.

Model Decisions & Exclusions
❌ Respiratory & GI disease load (yesterday's ED Dx) — Modifiers were derived against surge probability but found to be measuring volume autocorrelation (busy days follow busy days), not a disease-specific signal. ILI modifier already captures the respiratory illness signal from a cleaner source. Removed.
❌ Evening build-up model (18–21:00 cumulative arrivals) — Designed to warn about late-night surge before confirmation. Base late-night surge rates are too low (13% at 22:00) for the modifier to push bars above WATCH even at HIGH threshold. Staff already observe a busy department directly. Removed as dead weight.
❌ Weather (same-day) — Multivariate analysis confirmed weather lag effects were confounded by seasonality. Cold/rainy weather co-occurs with respiratory season; once month and ILI are controlled, ΔR²<0.001. The ILI modifier and empirical (hour × DOW × month) heatmap already capture the underlying seasonal signal. Intraday weather timing (e.g. afternoon rain → evening surge) also tested — not significant (RR=1.08, p=0.37).
❌ Barometric pressure lag — Same confounding mechanism. Pressure changes correlate with storm systems that cluster in winter respiratory season. No independent predictive value after ILI and month controlled.
❌ Moon phase — No significant effect detected (p=0.31).
❌ SNAP cycle — No significant effect (p=0.44).
❌ DST transitions — Single-hour artifact only; effect too small to model.
❌ Halloween (post-event lag) — n=3 post-event windows, too unstable for inclusion.
⚠️ Google Trends panel — Display-only. Not wired into surge probability (signal too indirect).
⚠️ IPS backlog inputs (12h/24h models) — Confirmed duplicate of backlog trend via delta-AUC testing. Removed from surge model.
Known Limitations
⚠️ Post-event lag modifiers calibrated at t=12–18/hr only. No validated signal below t=12.
⚠️ mPEDOCS reconstruction excludes observation/boarding patients not in Throughput.csv.
⚠️ All models trained on 2023–2025 data — performance may shift with operational or population changes.
⚠️ ILI data is weekly, not daily — same modifier applies all 7 days of the epiweek.
⚠️ Surge threshold is arrivals per hour, not census — high-acuity volume is not separately weighted.
⚠️ Disease load sliders require manual daily entry — not automated from Epic.
